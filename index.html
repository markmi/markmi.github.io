Examples of 4-week-interval WeeklyMeans vs. Weekly: Shows which is easier to read/interpret
(Note: N*(N WkInterval's WeeklyMean) gets the total for the N week interval involved.)

I'm generally limiting myself to contexts for which all the following age ranges seem reasonable: Age Ranges 85..99+, 10 year intervals, the lowest being 15..24, other than the Jurisdiction data source's age ranges 85..99+, 75..84, 65..74, 45..64, 25..44, 00..24. Other than that I avoid ages below 15. The data source for the Male/Female breakdown has 00..14, which mixes ages with wide differences.

Some breakdowns are just too sparse for this age range breakdown to be reasonable. For example, combining the NH-NatHawOthPaciIsl Female 15..19 and 20..24 Age Ranges (to form 15..24) has 5 MMWR week numbers with no 2015..2019 deaths listed: 18, 30, 32, 41, 42 and has 14 MMWR week numbers with only one death listed over those years. (I'll not list the MMWR week numbers.) The maximum death count for a MMWR week number over those years is 5 and only 3 MMWR week numbers have that count. Such leads to not having a NH-NatHawOthPaciIsl Male/Female age range breakdown. The NH-AmIndianALNat Female 15..24 age range has 45 of the 260 2015..2019 MMWR weeks missing --but I'm including the Male/Female age range breakdown for it.

The data source for the Male/Female breakdown does not have the NH-Multirace population data split out on its own. This leads to not having a NH-Multirace Male/Female age range breakdown.

I display the weekly Death Rates data via 4-week WeeklyMeans (5-weeks to span 2020's MMWR 53). This is to cut down the sizable noise for various group/age-range combinations for which the fraction of that population dying in each interval is much smaller and/or the population is much smaller, making the charts easier to read and interpret. N*[N Week WeeklyMean] gets the total for the N week interval. I will abbreviate my references to this by just explicitly mentioning 4 weeks generally --but the 5 week case is involved.

Some data sources may have the weeks with zero figures (so: week not missing), others may not have listed such a week for the category at all. When a population is small enough to have some weeks missing so that, for example, say, only 1 week is provided of a 4 week interval, the charts do not add in any missing weeks for how they are drawn.

The each data source for charts likely will cover somewhat different recent time frames: Only the Jurisdiction data is published weekly by the NCHS. The data for the 2 group breakdowns are only published on an irregular/Ad Hoc basis. Plus, I may not update things every time the data sources update:

I expect to update the Death Rates History charts very infrequently, especially for the period between the after-major-COVID-19-Wave plateau start in the data source records and the next major COVID-19 wave start in the records.

The pages generally allow picking what to display on the left vs. right sides, allowing for selective side-by-side comparisons but at least one page does not.

Death Rates History Charting/Plotting notes:

Note: The New York State Jurisdiction has the NYC Death data merged in. NYC is its own reporting jurisdiction for providing CDC death data, separate from New York State. But such is not true of most sources of population figures: New York State populations normally include the NYC population.

Given the large range for the fraction of the various populations that die each year based on the age ranges, the multi-age-range Death Rate Charts have the y-axis being logarithmic and so the charts would show constant multiplicative scale factors for y as constant distances between. The charts that are of a single Age Range are not logarithmic.

The death counts used were the CDC "Unweighted" counts for each Age Range referenced when weighted was also available.
The CDC MMWR 4-Week WeeklyMeans Maximums are from over 2015..2019 but also shown later for visual comparison purposes. (MMWR: Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report, weeks' data ending on Saturdays.)

I used the last year of population data that I had for the later years, such as 2020 data for 2021+ or, for NYC, 2019 data for 2020+.
